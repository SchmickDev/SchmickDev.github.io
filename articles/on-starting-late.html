On Starting Late

It took me a long time to "become" a developer. I initially went to college for music, specifically guitar. Then after not applying myself very well I was part of a somewhat successful band (at least successful enough to have toured the country). Then being in my early 20s with no college degree I turned to the restaurant industry. I very quickly realized that I did not want to remain in the restaurant industry as a career but lacked the motivation to do anything about it. After 7 years I finally returned to school. This time with the intention of getting a degree in history and becoming a school teacher. I attended my local community college earning my associates. After getting my associates I decided that I wanted to retire before I was 85 and I decided to switch majors. I remembered enjoying computer math in high school, and Amazon just announced that they were bringing thousands of jobs to my area. So I switched into Computer Science. I dont currently work at Amazon, but I am gainfully employed and happy with my job. Switching into computer science was one it was one of the best decisions that I have made for both me and my family. There are several things I have learned along the way which may be of use to someone. If not they can sit here as my ramblings as I rapidly approach old man yells at cloud.


Do You Need Prior Knowledge?

In short no. When I decided to change my major from history to Computer Science I had not programmed anything in about 13 years. To the point where I had forgotten anything that I had ever learned in relation to the subject. If you are thinking of switching careers (especially if you are going the college route) I would say that you probably should know whether or not you will sufficiently enjoy the field you are switching into. I can not speak the boot camp or self-taught route because I did not take those, but traditional Computer Science education is pretty heavy on Math in addition to the Computer Science courses. Before diving into a degree I would ask yourself if you have at least the ability to sit through material up to multivariate calculus, and maybe poke around a programming book to see if you enjoy programming and the theory behind it enough to go through a 4-year degree.


Do You Need a Wiz bang Computer?

Definitely not. I completed the majority of my degree on a 10.5" laptop with 4 gb of ram and a Intel Celeron processor (not that I recommend this). You may need some more powerful hardware for some of the more advanced courses but assuming that you have a computer made in the last decade it is probably sufficient to get through the majority of a Computer Science degree. If you have decided that you want to switch careers then do not let this be what holds you back.


* The one caveat to this is that some front end frameworks can be quite resource intensive while running development servers. For those instances a computer that was made 10 years ago may not cut it.


Time Management is King

Time management rules. As someone in my late 20s/early 30s attending school the most difficult classes were not those with difficult material, but rather those which had a large quantity of work. My networking class in particular was one of the most difficult classes which I took. Why? Because there was just a ton of work. None of the work was particularly difficult, but finding the time to fit in everything while managing other responsibilities was quite difficult. Working a full time job, while having other family commitments made time management one of the most important skills for my success upon returning to school. Along with this I found habits and dedicated study time to be incredibly important. I worked as a bartender Friday - Monday, all nights. This meant that Tuesday - Thursday I could study. Generally speaking I would work on things on Tuesday - Thursday for at least 4 hours a day and more if needed. If nothing was due I would work ahead because I did not have the luxury of working on a project for 12 hours a day for 7 days straight to meet an assignment deadline. When you have real world obligations like a house, a job, and family you have to ensure that those are met first and then you have time to fit school (or self study) in between the cracks. In order to maximize what you get out of this time management is key.


Motivation

The only factor that I would say was more important in my success was motivation. The drive to not allow myself to fail was imperative. I had been in my current employment situation for far too long and was finally sick of it, enough to actually do something about it. Enough where I wanted to go out and provide a better life for myself and my family. This meant I really could not fail. I did not have time too. If I failed a course that meant I would have to retake it, which meant ~$1500 and another semester in school. That was 6 more months that I would be stuck in the same position I was at. This was one of the key differences in me going back to school. I was far more motivated. I think this advice is important to anyone attending school, or attempting to learn something new. Finding motivation is key. Finding some reason why you will continue to work towards a goal is extremely important to success regardless of what that goal is. I am not going to tell you where to find your motivation. I can tell you that contrary to a lot of tech influencers it does not necessarily have to be the love of programming. Now I think if you can not stand programming and you absolutely hate it then it is probably not a great idea to just get into it for the money, but if you like it well enough I think something like providing a better life for you and your family can be sufficient motivation to succeed. If you are younger, chase your passions, you have time to fail.


Reasonable Expectations

As someone older switching into the tech industry you have some advantages. You have life experience. Ideally you have non tech related workplace experience, showing that you know how to function as an employee. When applying to jobs I put the entirety of my work history down despite none of it being related to computers. I got more than one interview where the interviewer or recruiter told me that my resume was selected because it was interesting. Not because I had the most impressive projects, not because I graduated from the most impressive school but because I had an interesting resume. It showed life experience and that I knew how to function in a workplace. If you are someone older getting into tech these things can set you apart from other candidates, especially your peers who are also just graduating.


On the flip side once you do get a job impostor syndrome is still a real thing. But you have come this far, so why stop learning now? One of the difficulties that I have encountered is finding time to accelerate my learning. If I were 22 fresh out of school even with a full time job I would be able to sink an extra 20 hours a week into learning and honing my programming skills. But as someone who is a little older this may be harder to do. I'm in my mid 30s and have many of the responsibilities that people in their mid 30s have. Taking care of the house, talking the kids to birthday parties, changing the oil in my wife's car. I would not change any of these, but the reality is that these take time, and if you have less time then someone (most likely younger than you) will pass you by in skill and possibly position. That's something that I have had to figure out how to deal with. I will probably not be the next John Carmack, I will probably not be the best programmer at my company, and I will probably not contribute anything meaningful to the field of Computer Science. None of these things stop me from being good at my job or being happy, and none of these things change the fact that I am in a better place after having switched careers. In up scaling yourself see point 1 Time Management is King. As it turns out this did not really change after I graduated.


You Know Nothing

Ok, you graduated, got a job, showed up on your first day, and then your coworkers start talking. What are they talking about? what is GraphQL and where does it live? How did I get a full stack development job? What's a React component? What is a JSP file and why does it look like if was invented to confuse me? Why are there so many acronyms? What doe these acronyms mean? Why are there acronyms for things that probably should not have acronyms? WHY DOES JavaScript NOT KNOW HOW TO SORT AN ARRAY OF NUMBERS? Can someone please explain this to me like I'm 5? Very quickly you will realize that there are parts, sometimes entire sections of the tech stack which you just have no clue about. This is fine, It is normal. The tech landscape at this point is so wide that it is impossible to know everything even 20 years into your career. The flip side of this is that when you are first starting with practical application of what you have learned, you really do not know anything. One of the big shocks to me was when I pulled cloned my first back end service repo. The back end for my company is written in Java, which is a language that I was familiar with. To my shock it looked completely alien. I had not seen functional Java before and the entirety of the streams API was new to me. Thankfully due to some functional programming classes that which I had taken I was able to pick up that almost immediately. In addition to that there were annotations everywhere, what they did was a complete mystery, as was the method in which they worked (reflection). So I started researching and asking questions. Asking someone for a general lay of the land and what technologies that we use was critical. From there I would just read the source code and google when I encountered a problem which I didn't know the answer to. If it was something structural/architectural or related to an internal framework I would ask someone my senior. I spent time learning everything I could to get me up to speed. Almost 2 years in I would say that I finally have a reasonably firm grasp of the core tech stack. One of the pieces of advice that I most recently heard which really resonated with me is from Mark Richards who recommends trying to expand what you know you dont know. Develop your core competencies in your direct domain. Additionally start asking questions about how things work that are not things your are directly working on. Not because you are going to get an in depth answer and know everything about it, but because when/if you have to work with it in the future you will know that you need to look into it, as opposed to not even knowing it existed.


Final Thoughts

In short I will probably never make a computer in minecraft, or completely redo Ocarina of Time to play at 60 fps on original hardware. It is likely that my projects outside of work are relatively unremarkable, at least for a while. But that is ok. My decision to switch careers was about making a positive move for myself and my family. Not an attempt to be the best programmer in the world or do something truely incredible.
